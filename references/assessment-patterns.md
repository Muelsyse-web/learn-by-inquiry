# Assessment Patterns

Use this reference when creating formative checks, quizzes, practice tasks, and feedback.

## Default Feedback Loop

1. Ask a short diagnostic prompt.
2. Require the learner to explain reasoning when feasible.
3. Identify the useful part of the answer.
4. Name the gap, misconception, or missing evidence.
5. Give a minimal correction or hint.
6. Ask a follow-up that tests the corrected idea in a new context.

## Prompt Patterns

### Prediction

"Before I explain, what do you predict will happen if [condition changes]? Why?"

Use for causal models, systems, math, science, programming, economics, and psychology.

### Explain Why

"Explain why [claim] is true in your own words. Avoid using the term itself in the explanation."

Use for conceptual understanding and circular-definition detection.

### Compare and Contrast

"What is the key difference between A and B? Give one case where confusing them would lead to a wrong answer."

Use for adjacent concepts, common misconceptions, theories, and methods.

### Evidence Check

"What observation, example, or source would support this claim? What would weaken it?"

Use for research literacy, critical thinking, and source evaluation.

### Transfer Case

"Apply the idea to this new case: [case]. What stays the same, and what changes?"

Use after an explanation or worked example.

### Misconception Probe

"A learner says: '[plausible misconception]'. What is tempting about this answer, and what is wrong with it?"

Use when a topic has a known trap.

### Self-Rating With Evidence

"Rate your confidence from 1-5, then give the reason for your rating. What evidence would move you one point higher?"

Use for metacognition and deciding the next support level.

## Feedback Templates

### Mostly Correct

"Your core idea is right: [specific part]. The next refinement is [specific nuance]. Try this transfer check: [question]."

### Partly Correct

"You are tracking [productive idea], but the gap is [specific gap]. A better rule is [compact rule]. Test it here: [question]."

### Misconception

"That answer is tempting because [why it seems plausible]. The problem is [specific contradiction or missing condition]. Use this distinction instead: [distinction]. Now apply it to [new case]."

### Insufficient Evidence

"The claim may be plausible, but the evidence you gave only supports [weaker claim]. What would you need to observe or compare to support the stronger claim?"

## Quiz Design Rules

- Prefer 1-3 questions per turn.
- Mix one recall or definition check with one reasoning or transfer check when appropriate.
- Do not provide answer keys before the learner attempts the questions unless they request self-study mode.
- Avoid trick questions unless the goal is misconception diagnosis.
- For exam preparation, ask whether to use exam-like format, but keep feedback formative.
- When the learner answers, adapt the next question rather than continuing a fixed quiz.
