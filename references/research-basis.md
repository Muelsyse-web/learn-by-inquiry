# Research Basis

Use this reference when the task needs the educational rationale behind the skill, citations, or guardrails for inquiry-based learning.

## Core Claims

- Inquiry-based learning is not simply "go search and learn." It organizes learning around questions, evidence, explanation, argument, reflection, and revision.
- A useful generic cycle is: orientation, conceptualization, investigation, conclusion, and discussion/reflection. Discussion includes both communication and reflection and can appear throughout the cycle.
- Guided inquiry is safer and usually more effective than minimally guided discovery, especially for novices.
- Scaffolding should adapt to the learner: give more structure for unfamiliar topics, then gradually remove support as the learner can generate questions, methods, explanations, and checks independently.
- Formative assessment is part of inquiry. It reveals current models, misconceptions, evidence gaps, and transfer ability.

## Design Implications

- Diagnose before teaching. Identify whether the learner needs orientation, conceptual framing, investigation support, explanation, transfer, or reflection.
- Keep inquiry authentic. Ask questions that require reasoning, evidence, comparison, counterexamples, or application rather than superficial recall.
- Avoid cognitive overload. Do not ask a novice to invent the whole path, search space, method, and evaluation criteria at once.
- Use direct explanation as scaffolding, not as the default endpoint. Explanation should prepare the learner to reason or act.
- Treat uncertainty explicitly. Ask what evidence would support or weaken a claim.

## Failure Modes to Avoid

- Pure discovery: asking the learner to figure everything out with little guidance.
- Quiz-only tutoring: measuring recall without changing the learning path.
- Explanation-only tutoring: giving a polished lecture without eliciting the learner's model.
- Vague curiosity prompts: asking broad questions that do not create progress.
- Fake inquiry: presenting a predetermined answer while pretending the learner is discovering it.
- Over-scaffolding: making every step so guided that the learner never forms questions, hypotheses, or criteria.
